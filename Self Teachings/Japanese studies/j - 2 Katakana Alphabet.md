

$\text{Base katakana Monographs}:\begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c} &\texttt{a} & \texttt{i} & \texttt{u} & \texttt{e} & \texttt{o}\\ \hline \varnothing & ア & イ & ウ & エ & オ\\ \texttt{K} & カ & キ & ク & ケ & コ  \\ \texttt{S} & サ & シ & ス & セ & ソ \\ \texttt{T} & タ & チ & ツ & テ & ト  \\ \texttt{N} & ナ & ニ & ヌ & ネ & ノ \\ \texttt{H} & ハ & ヒ & フ & ヘ & ホ  \\ \texttt{M} & マ &ミ&ム&メ&モ \\ \texttt{Y} & ヤ &&ユ &&ヨ \\ \texttt{R} & ラ & リ & ル & レ & ロ  \\ \texttt{W} & ワ&&&&ヲ\end{array}$  $\begin{array}{c|c|c} \texttt{ya}&\texttt{yu}&\texttt{yo}\\ \hline \text{ } \\ キャ & キュ & キョ  \\ シャ & シュ & ショ  \\  チャ & チュ & チョ \\ ニャ & ニュ & ニョ  \\ ヒャ & ヒュ & ヒョ  \\ ミャ & ミュ & ミョ  \\  \\ リャ & リュ & リョ \\ &\end{array}\text{Basic Digraphs}$ 

on the $\texttt{W}$ row, only ワ and ヲ are used, on the $\texttt{Y}$ row, only ヤ, ユ and ヨ are used

<b>シ is pronounced "shi",チ is ponouced "chi", ツ is pronounced "tsu", フ is pronounced "fu"</b> 

$\text{Base monograph diacritics} :\begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c} &\texttt{a} & \texttt{i} & \texttt{u} & \texttt{e} & \texttt{o}\\ \hline  \texttt{G} & ガ & ギ & グ & ゲ & ゴ  \\ \texttt{Z} & ザ & ジ & ズ & ゼ & ゾ \\ \texttt{D} & ダ & ヂ & ヅ & デ & ド  \\ \texttt{B} & バ & ビ & ブ & ベ & ボ  \\ \texttt{P} & パ & ピ & プ & ペ & ポ\end{array}$ $\begin{array}{c|c|c} \texttt{ya}  & \texttt{yu}  & \texttt{yo}\\ \hline ギャ & ギュ & ギョ  \\ ジャ & ジュ & ジョ  \\ ヂャ & ヂュ & ヂョ \\ ビャ & ビュ & ビョ  \\ ピャ & ピュ & ピョ\end{array}$ Digraphs
ヂ(ji) & ヅ(zu) are used more to pronounce foreign words

<b>ジ is pronounced "ji"</b> 

With all that, there is :
 - the ン (n) sound, which is a vowel by himself 
 - the ッ which indicates consonant lengthening, similar to double consonants in Latin based languages
 - the ー which indicates a longer vowel, similar to adding vowels in Latin based languages

⚠ <b>Be careful not to mistake シ(shi),ソ(so),ツ(tsu),ン(n)</b>
